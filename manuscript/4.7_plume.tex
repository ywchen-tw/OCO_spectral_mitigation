\subsection{Plume preservation and correction safety}
\label{sec:result-plume}



Validation with TCCON stations and additional comparisons with airborne and shipborne measurements demonstrate the ability of the \emph{DE} model correction. The concerns of changes in far-cloud footprints are also minor. However, if the correction changes the \xco enhancement of emission-source-affected footprints, it could propagate to emission flux rate calculation. That is to say, a correction that removes near-cloud biases from \xco must not remove real, localized \ce{CO2} enhancements together with it. Because the model is trained to delete short-scale anomalies, this is the most direct safety concern for downstream emission applications, and we test it on the power-plant overpasses of the \citet{nassar2017power_plants_oco2} catalog that fall in our processed set (ten usable overpasses of eight plants; inventory in Appendix~F, Table~F1). For each overpass, we compare the along-track window around the plant against matched plume-free control windows on the same orbit, at least 100~km from any cataloged plant. If the correction were deleting plumes, the removal inside the plant window should exceed the removal in its matched controls.



Three results establish that the correction is plume-safe. First, the clearest enhancement in the set survives. Over the Westar plant (26 June 2023, clear sky, nearest cloud $\approx$ 50~km) the
$\sim$$+0.6$~ppm enhancement at closest approach is essentially unchanged by the correction (Fig.~\ref{fig:plume-preservation}a). Second, the matched control-window null passes in five of the seven
testable windows, and the two flagged windows are identified as cloud artifacts rather than plumes by a band-resolved discriminator (Fig.~\ref{fig:plume-preservation}b). A real \ce{CO2} enhancement can
only move $\langle l'\rangle$ in the \ce{CO2} bands, by approximately $\langle l'\rangle\,\Delta$\xco/\xco through the prior-based optical depth, while the \ce{O2}A band must stay flat (\ce{O2} is insensitive to \ce{CO2}). In both flagged windows, the \ce{O2}A band shifts as strongly as the \ce{CO2} bands, presenting the all-band influence of clouds, consistent with their sub-kilometer nearest-cloud distances. Third, an upper bound on the plume signal that the spectral channel could remove, obtained by propagating the observed <$l'$> shifts through the background sensitivity $\mathrm{d}\mu/\mathrm{d}<l'>$, is at most 0.21~ppm in the worst tested window and below 0.01~ppm in the clear cases — small compared with the 0.6--2.5~ppm plant-window enhancements in the catalog. The full transect set and the per-case bounds are given in Appendix~F (Figs.~F2--F3, Tables~F2--F3), so the four cases shown here are not a selection.


The control windows also capture the smoothing that does occur. In plume-free windows, the full model removes approximately 55\,\% [40--63\,\%] of the local \xco spread; retraining without the spectral features removes essentially the same amount (54\,\%), whereas retraining without the retrieval-state (\xco-derived) features removes only 29\,\%. The local smoothing is therefore primarily attributable to the retrieval-state channel, not to the photon path-length features. The spectral channel contributes discrimination (the band-resolved fingerprint above) rather than amplitude, which is the desirable division of labor for plume safety (attribution table in Appendix~F, Table~F3).
